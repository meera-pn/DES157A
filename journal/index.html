Overuse of Overlays - Response to This Article

11/19/24

Overlays are very helpful, but they should only be used when appropriate. The article goes over the places to use (and not use) overlays.

One example that the article uses is overlays on mobile devices. Generally, if an overlay is used on mobile, it will fill the screen and may be unusable or un-exitable due to size. This is something that I have definitely experienced in my own life, so it was interesting to see it laid out here. The article also talks about the actions that users will be taking from the overlays. Filling out forms, displaying user requested information, or advertising a deal are all valid ways to use overlays, but designers should make sure that they don't interrupt the overall flow of the UX and that they are easy to dismiss.

Game Design Research

11/14/24

The main thing that I learned from this article was the idea of using design to direct the user to do things that you want them to do. Something the article mentions a lot is putting options that the designers want users to choose in easy to reach areas, and the non-desirable options in difficult to reach areas. This direction can also be done through color, making a “rate” button bright green and eye-catching and an “ignore” button a more subdued blue, with less emphasis placed on it.

This relates to the idea of clearly showing the user which actions give certain results, like a pop-up animation to show which button triggered the pop-up. This is important because sometimes, when I do something online, I trigger something helpful and I don’t know how I did it, so I can’t access it again.

One thing that stood out to me was the idea of emotional connection, though I think this would really only work with character based games. When asking someone to rate the game, it says to have a character looking at them as part of the overlay design. I think this is interesting because it really depends on the user feeling like they have some sort of relationship with the character, where they want to help it out.

Form Design - Response to This Article

10/14/24

The main takeaway that I had from the article was that the process of filling out a form is generally considered unpleasant, and it is the designers job to keep it as easy and engaging as possible. This is generally done by making it easy to fill out and easy to navigate, and providing clear feedback to user input.

Many people will not finish completing a form if it is too long or difficult to fill out, and a designer wants to mitigate that when designing one. This can be done by putting easier prompts (like their name) first in the form, and leaving the complex ones for the end. If people are given easy prompts first, they are more likely to fill out difficult prompts later because they feel committed to finishing. This can also be done by accurate labels and placeholder text, making optional and required fields clear, providing a matching keyboard on mobile, adding a “show password” button, and other attributes that make a form easier to fill out.
